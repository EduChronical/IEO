Gasketless High-Pressure PEM Electrolyzer Bipolar Plate with Laser-Induced Periodic Surface Structures and Dissimilar-Metal Friction-Stir Welded Sealing

The following specification particularly describes the invention and the manner in which it is to be performed.

Abstract

A gasketless bipolar plate for proton exchange membrane (PEM) electrolyzers comprises micro-structured titanium foil metallurgically sealed to an aluminum frame via friction-stir welding. Laser-induced periodic surface structures (LIPSS) with ridge height 80–150 µm and periodicity 5–15 µm generate micro-vortices for passive bubble detachment, reducing bubble residence time to 50–100 ms. The dissimilar-metal joint forms an intermetallic layer <0.5 µm thick, providing hermetic sealing >50 bar differential pressure and electrical isolation >10⁴ Ω·cm². Continuous roll-to-roll manufacturing integrates laser micro-structuring and in-line friction-stir welding at 5–10 m min⁻¹. The invention eliminates elastomeric gaskets, enabling 50–80 bar operation for green hydrogen production.

1Field of the Invention

The present invention relates to the field of electrochemical hydrogen production, specifically to improved bipolar plates for proton exchange membrane (PEM) water electrolyzers. More particularly, the invention concerns gasketless sealing systems, surface micro-structuring for two-phase flow management, and continuous manufacturing methods for high-pressure electrolyzer stacks operating at differential pressures exceeding 50 bar.

2Background of the Invention and Prior Art

2.1 Technical Background

Proton exchange membrane (PEM) water electrolysis represents a critical technology for green hydrogen production, offering high current density, rapid response to renewable energy fluctuations, and pure hydrogen output. However, conventional PEM electrolyzers face significant limitations when scaling to high-pressure operation (>30 bar), primarily due to sealing challenges, bubble-induced performance degradation, and manufacturing complexity.

2.2 Problems with Existing Technology

Current state-of-the-art bipolar plates rely on elastomeric gaskets (typically PTFE, FKM, or EPDM) compressed between flat metal plates to maintain hermetic sealing. These gaskets suffer from: (i) permeation losses at high differential pressures, requiring frequent replacement; (ii) chemical degradation in the acidic, oxidative PEM environment (pH 2–3, oxygen evolution potential >1.23 V vs. SHE); (iii) thermal cycling fatigue causing compression set; and (iv) complex stack assembly requiring precise torque control and tie-rod structures.

Additionally, conventional bipolar plates utilize millimeter-scale flow fields (serpentine or interdigitated) where hydrogen and oxygen bubbles coalesce and adhere to surfaces, creating ohmic losses and mass transport limitations. Bubble residence times typically exceed 500 ms in conventional designs, reducing effective active area and increasing cell voltage by 50–100 mV at 2 A cm⁻².

2.4 Novelty Statement

The present invention addresses the critical unmet need for gasketless, high-pressure PEM electrolyzer technology by synergistically combining: (i) laser-induced periodic surface structures (LIPSS) specifically engineered for micro-vortex bubble detachment in electrolysis conditions; (ii) dissimilar-metal friction-stir welding between titanium active layers and aluminum frames to create hermetic, electrically isolated seals without gaskets; and (iii) continuous roll-to-roll manufacturing integrating these processes at industrially relevant speeds (5–10 m min⁻¹). This combination is neither disclosed nor suggested in the prior art.

3Objects of the Invention

It is an object of the present invention to provide a bipolar plate assembly that eliminates elastomeric gaskets while maintaining hermetic sealing at differential pressures of 50–80 bar.

It is another object to reduce bubble residence time on electrode surfaces to 50–100 ms through passive micro-vortex generation, thereby reducing ohmic losses and improving current distribution.

It is a further object to provide electrical isolation >10⁴ Ω·cm² between adjacent cells through controlled intermetallic layer formation, preventing galvanic corrosion in the PEM environment.

It is yet another object to enable continuous, high-throughput manufacturing of complex bipolar plates via roll-to-roll integration of laser micro-structuring and friction-stir welding.

4Summary of the Invention

According to the present invention, there is provided a gasketless bipolar plate for PEM electrolyzers comprising: a micro-structured titanium foil having laser-induced periodic surface structures (LIPSS) on the active surface, metallurgically sealed to an aluminum alloy frame via friction-stir welding (FSW) along the perimeter. The LIPSS comprise parallel ridges with height 80–150 µm, periodicity 5–15 µm, and aspect ratio 3:1 to 5:1, oriented perpendicular to the electrolyte flow direction to generate counter-rotating micro-vortices that detach gas bubbles within 50–100 ms. The FSW joint forms an intermetallic layer <0.5 µm thick, comprising primarily TiAl₃ phase, providing hermetic sealing against hydrogen/oxygen permeation and electrical resistance >10⁴ Ω·cm² to prevent galvanic corrosion.

5.2 Laser-Induced Periodic Surface Structures (LIPSS)

The active surface of the titanium foil (110) is patterned with LIPSS using femtosecond or picosecond laser pulses (wavelength 1030 nm, pulse duration 200–500 fs, fluence 0.5–2.0 J cm⁻²). The laser parameters are selected to produce low-spatial-frequency LIPSS (LSFL) with the following critical dimensions:

  • Ridge height (h): 80–150 µm, preferably 100–120 µm. Heights <80 µm generate insufficient vortex strength for bubble detachment; heights >150 µm increase pressure drop excessively (>0.5 bar across the active area).
  • Periodicity (Λ): 5–15 µm, preferably 8–12 µm. This range matches the capillary length of water at 80 °C (λc ≈ 2.7 mm) scaled by the vortex Strouhal number (St ≈ 0.2), ensuring resonance between vortex shedding and bubble departure frequency.
  • Aspect ratio (h/Λ): 3:1 to 5:1. Ratios <3:1 produce weak vortices; ratios >5:1 cause vortex breakdown and flow separation.
  • Orientation: Ridges perpendicular to electrolyte flow direction (±5° tolerance) to maximize the transverse pressure gradient driving bubble detachment.

The LIPSS generate counter-rotating micro-vortices with characteristic diameter dv ≈ 0.2Λ and shedding frequency fv = St·U/Λ, where U is the superficial velocity (0.1–0.5 m s⁻¹). These vortices create a time-averaged wall shear stress τw ≈ 0.5ρU²Cf (where Cf ≈ 0.02 for LIPSS surfaces) that overcomes bubble adhesion forces Fadhesion = πdbσ(1+cosθ), where db is bubble diameter (typically 50–200 µm in PEM electrolyzers), σ is surface tension (0.043 N m⁻¹ at 80 °C), and θ is contact angle (<30° on laser-structured titanium).

5.3 Dissimilar-Metal Friction-Stir Welded Joint

The hermetic seal between the titanium foil (110) and aluminum frame (120) is achieved via friction-stir welding (FSW), a solid-state joining process that avoids melting and associated formation of brittle intermetallic phases. The FSW parameters are critically controlled to:

Parameter Value Range Critical Function
Tool rotation speed 800–1500 rpm Generates frictional heat for plasticization without melting (Ti melting point 1668 °C, Al 660 °C)
Traverse speed 100–300 mm min⁻¹ Controls heat input and intermetallic layer thickness; higher speeds reduce IMC growth
Tool offset 0.5–1.0 mm toward Al side Positions the shear zone in the softer Al alloy, minimizing TiAl₃ formation at interface
Axial force 8–15 kN Ensures full penetration while preventing flash formation and oxide entrapment
Tool material Tungsten carbide (WC-Co) or PCBN Withstands abrasive Ti-Al mixture at 400–500 °C process temperature

The resulting joint features an intermetallic compound (IMC) layer (170) with thickness <0.5 µm, verified by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS). The IMC comprises predominantly TiAl₃ phase (D022 structure, tetragonal, resistivity ≈ 50 µΩ·cm) with minor TiAl and Ti₃Al regions. This thickness is critical: layers >0.5 µm exhibit micro-cracking under thermal cycling (ΔT = 80 °C during startup/shutdown), compromising hermeticity; layers <0.1 µm provide insufficient electrical isolation.

Electrical isolation mechanism: The TiAl₃ layer creates a potential barrier φb ≈ 0.8 eV at the Ti/Al interface, resulting in contact resistance Rc >10⁴ Ω·cm² as measured by four-point probe method (ASTM F43). This prevents galvanic coupling between the titanium anode (E° = −1.63 V vs. SHE for Ti/TiO₂) and aluminum frame (E° = −1.66 V vs. SHE), eliminating galvanic corrosion that would otherwise occur in the acidic PEM environment (pH 2–3).

5.4 Hermetic Sealing Performance

The FSW joint achieves hermetic sealing against hydrogen and oxygen permeation, validated by helium leak testing (ASTM E498) and electrochemical hydrogen permeation testing (ISO 17081). The leak rate is <1×10⁻⁹ mbar·L·s⁻¹·cm⁻² at 80 bar differential pressure, satisfying the requirements for high-pressure PEM electrolyzers (target: <1×10⁻⁸ mbar·L·s⁻¹·cm⁻²). The joint withstands 10,000 pressure cycles (0–80 bar, 1 Hz frequency) without degradation, demonstrating fatigue resistance superior to elastomeric gaskets (typically fail at 1000–2000 cycles).

6.2 Critical Process Control Parameters

Laser Structuring Station (220): The titanium foil (200) passes under a femtosecond laser (1030 nm, 500 fs, 100 kHz repetition rate) with galvo-scanning optics producing LIPSS across the 250 cm² active area in 8–12 seconds (corresponding to 5 m min⁻¹ line speed). The laser fluence is maintained at 1.0±0.2 J cm⁻² by real-time power monitoring and beam expander adjustment. The structured foil passes through a deionized water rinse (230) to remove ablation debris, followed by nitrogen drying.

Friction-Stir Welding Station (250): Pre-formed aluminum frames (240) are presented from above the moving titanium web using a pick-and-place mechanism synchronized to line speed. The FSW tool (WC-Co, shoulder diameter 12 mm, probe length 2.5 mm) engages the overlap region (Ti foil atop Al frame edge) with the following controlled parameters:

  • Tool positioning: Automated vision alignment (±0.1 mm precision) ensures the tool centerline is offset 0.7±0.2 mm toward the aluminum side.
  • Force control: Axial force maintained at 12±1 kN via load cell feedback, with Z-axis compensation for titanium thickness variation (±0.01 mm).
  • Temperature monitoring: Infrared pyrometer (3–5 µm wavelength) monitors weld zone temperature at 450±30 °C; excessive temperature triggers automatic parameter adjustment to prevent IMC growth.
  • Weld quality verification: Eddy current testing (260) with 500 kHz probe detects surface-breaking defects >50 µm; rejected parts are marked for manual inspection.

Technical Rationale for FSW Integration: Conventional FSW is a batch process with typical traverse speeds of 100–500 mm min⁻¹. The present invention achieves 5–10 m min⁻¹ (5000–10,000 mm min⁻¹) through: (i) reduced plunge depth (2.0 mm vs. 3–4 mm conventional) enabled by the thin titanium foil; (ii) high-rpm spindle (1500 rpm vs. 600–1000 rpm) reducing heat input per unit length; and (iii) linear weld geometry (perimeter seal) eliminating complex tool paths. The heat input per unit length Q = (2π/60)·ω·T·(vtraverse)⁻¹ is maintained at 0.3–0.5 kJ mm⁻¹, comparable to conventional FSW despite the 10× speed increase, by proportionally increasing rotation speed ω and reducing torque T through optimized tool geometry.

Best Mode for Carrying Out the Invention

The best mode known to the inventors for carrying out the invention at the time of filing is as follows: A Grade 1 titanium foil (0.2 mm thickness) is laser-structured with LIPSS having ridge height 100 µm, periodicity 10 µm, and aspect ratio 4:1, oriented perpendicular to flow. The structured foil is friction-stir welded to a 6061-T6 aluminum frame (2.5 mm thickness) using a tungsten carbide tool at 1200 rpm, 200 mm min⁻¹ traverse speed, 0.7 mm offset toward aluminum, and 12 kN axial force, producing an intermetallic layer thickness of 0.3–0.4 µm. This configuration achieves hermetic sealing at 80 bar with electrical isolation >2×10⁴ Ω·cm² and bubble residence time <100 ms, as validated by 1000-hour continuous operation testing.

8Industrial Applicability

The invention is directly applicable to the manufacturing of high-pressure PEM electrolyzers for green hydrogen production. The continuous roll-to-roll process enables cost reduction to <$50/kW at 100 MW annual production capacity, compared to >$150/kW for conventional machined bipolar plates with gaskets. The elimination of elastomeric gaskets extends stack lifetime from 20,000–30,000 hours to >60,000 hours, critical for achieving levelized cost of hydrogen (LCOH) targets of <$2/kg. The technology is transferable to other electrochemical devices requiring high-pressure sealing and two-phase flow management, including PEM fuel cells, CO₂ electrolyzers, and redox flow batteries.

laser micro-structuring said titanium foil with LIPSS comprising parallel ridges of height 80–150 µm and periodicity 5–15 µm using femtosecond or picosecond laser pulses;

presenting pre-formed aluminum alloy frames to said structured titanium foil; and

friction-stir welding said aluminum alloy frames to said titanium foil along the perimeter with tool rotation speed of 800–1500 rpm, traverse speed of 100–300 mm min⁻¹, and tool offset of 0.5–1.0 mm toward said aluminum alloy, thereby forming an intermetallic layer of thickness less than 0.5 µm.
